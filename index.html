Big Tech Club

Speculative Essay

The Developer Directory That Doesn't Exist Yet

On why the current options for developer identity online fall short, what a simpler professional landing page with optional skill discovery could look like, and why it only works as part of a community that already exists.

March 2026

Part I

The Problem with Developer Identity Online

There is no good place for a software engineer to simply exist on the internet. GitHub is a version control host that doubles, awkwardly, as a portfolio — and it does both jobs with obvious strain. A developer who has spent years building complex systems inside a private corporate repository appears, to the outside world, as if they have done nothing at all. Their contribution graph is a flatline. Their pinned repositories are side projects from half a decade ago. GitHub does show private contribution activity — a green square is a green square — but it communicates effort, not skill. It says nothing about what you built, how hard it was, or what you know.

LinkedIn is useful for what it was built to do: work history, professional connections, the broad surface area of a résumé made searchable. Most engineers maintain a profile there and probably always will. But LinkedIn is a poor answer to a different question — how do you present yourself as a practitioner, with specific skills at specific levels, in a way that someone technical can quickly read and act on? Its skill system lets you list technologies, but caps the display at five and offers no way to express depth or seniority within each one. Its search tools, even for paying recruiters, match on keywords rather than structured competence claims. The platform optimizes for reach and engagement, not for the kind of precise, low-noise signal that makes skill-based discovery actually work.

Portfolio tools and identity experiments have tried to thread this needle — read.cv was a genuine attempt before it was acquired and wound down — but none solved the underlying structural problem: they were standalone products with no built-in community, no critical mass, and no clear reason for engineers to show up and stay.

The most specific failure, though, is something none of these platforms addressed at all. No mainstream tool supports self-reported skill levels in a structured, filterable way. You can list technologies on LinkedIn, but you cannot say you are a senior-level Elixir engineer with intermediate Rust and a year of production Phoenix experience. That granularity — the kind that actually helps someone decide whether to reach out — simply does not exist anywhere in a form designed for discovery.

Stack Overflow's annual developer survey captures self-reported skill data at scale — but in aggregate, not as individual profiles. The signal exists; there is just no surface to make it discoverable.
Part II

A Landing Page, Not a Social Network

What if there were a calm, permanent place online where a developer could simply present themselves — without the overhead of maintaining a personal website, and without the noise of a social platform wrapped around them? A page you could link to from anywhere: a Twitter bio, an email signature, a conference talk slide. Something that says, clearly and quietly, here is who I am and what I do.

The minimum viable version of this is close to what Linktree already offers: a photo, a short bio, and a collection of links to wherever else you exist online — your GitHub, a personal site, a portfolio, a project you are proud of. That alone is more useful than most people's current options for a professional landing page, which is either a LinkedIn profile surrounded by algorithmic noise or a personal website that takes time to build and maintain.

The additional value comes from optional structure. If you choose to list your skills and note your overall level, you become findable in ways you were not before. Someone looking for a senior Elixir engineer in your city can filter to you specifically — not because you applied for something, but because you exist in a directory that makes that search possible. You do not need to fill out the structured fields to have a useful page. But if you do, the page becomes more than a landing pad — it becomes a signal.

The profile data should be yours to take with you. Exportable, deletable, not held hostage to a platform's survival or business model pivot. Engineers who have watched tools they relied on get acquired and wound down know the cost of building on someone else's infrastructure. This is not a technical guarantee so much as a design commitment — a statement about whose interests the platform actually serves.

Part III

The Shape of the Thing

The profile has two layers, and only the first is required. The base layer is a personal landing page: photo, name, location, a short bio, and links — to GitHub, a personal site, a portfolio, whatever you want to point people toward. This alone is useful. It takes ten minutes to fill out and gives you a clean, permanent URL to put in a bio or an email signature.

The optional layer is what makes the directory interesting. If you choose to declare your overall skill level and list the technologies you work with — each tagged with your self-assessed competence — you become searchable in a structured way. A hiring manager or a fellow developer can filter by location, technology, skill level, and availability, and find profiles that actually match what they are looking for. The filters are the product. You opt into them by filling in the fields; you opt out simply by leaving them blank.

Everything is self-reported. There is no verification system, no endorsement mechanism, no badge that proves you are what you say you are. The incentive for honesty is structural — overstating your level leads to conversations you are not prepared for — and the form design does more to establish trust than any machinery of attestation would. Linking to public work is always an option, and speaks for itself without requiring a formal verification step.

Profile pages are public and indexable by default. Engineers should be findable through search engines, not just within the platform. Contact details are gated to registered users, which is sufficient friction to deter most automated harvesting without walling off the directory from the people it is actually meant to serve.

Part IV

Why This Is Not a Standalone Product

A developer directory with no members is worth nothing. That is the whole problem. LinkedIn's moat is not its features — its features are widely disliked — it is the accumulated presence of hundreds of millions of professionals who are already there. Building a competing directory from scratch, without an existing audience, is a cold-start problem that no amount of good design solves on its own.

This is a secondary product. It works when it is attached to a community that already has the trust of working engineers — one where developers show up regularly, identify with the brand, and have already established some shared context about what they know and how they work. The directory becomes valuable the moment that community's membership is large enough and specific enough that the filters produce meaningful results. It does not need to compete with LinkedIn globally; it needs to be genuinely useful within a defined population.

The business model follows from this logic. For individual engineers, the profile is free — permanently, not as a loss-leader. The value to the host organization is different: a searchable, curated directory of their community's members is something employers will pay for access to, particularly when they already trust the community's signal. That is a cleaner transaction than a recruiter's commission, and it keeps the incentive structure legible to everyone involved.

The precedent is media companies that built subscriber-only communities and found, over time, that the community itself was more valuable than the content that seeded it. The directory is that community's professional surface — made useful to the outside world without compromising what made it worth joining.

The right organization to build this already has most of what it needs: an engaged membership of developers who trust it, a reputation built on quality rather than scale, and — if it has been paying attention — inbound interest from employers who want to hire from that specific pool. The missing piece is not technical. It is the decision to offer a structured professional surface to a community that has, until now, existed only as an audience.

Whether that decision gets made is not a product question. It is a strategic one. But the engineers are already there, the employers are already asking, and the gap between those two facts is exactly the size of this idea.
