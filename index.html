Big Tech Club

Speculative Essay

The Developer Directory That Doesn't Exist Yet

On why GitHub profiles obscure more than they reveal, why LinkedIn is the wrong answer to the right question, and what a simpler, more honest developer identity layer might look like — and who is already positioned to build it.

March 2026

Part I

The Problem with Developer Identity Online

There is no good place for a software engineer to simply exist on the internet. GitHub is a version control host that doubles, awkwardly, as a portfolio — and it does both jobs with obvious strain. A developer who has spent years building complex systems inside a private corporate repository appears, to the outside world, as if they have done nothing at all. Their contribution graph is a flatline. Their pinned repositories are side projects from half a decade ago. GitHub does show private contribution activity — a green square is a green square — but it communicates effort, not skill. It says nothing about what you built, how hard it was, or what you know.

LinkedIn fills the gap in theory, but not in practice. Engineers distrust it — specifically because it is a social network first and a professional directory second. It demands constant participation, surfaces the wrong kind of visibility, and forces professional identity into a format designed for résumés and recruiting pipelines rather than for honest communication of craft. The notifications, the engagement loops, the algorithmic feed — all of it is machinery built to serve the platform, not the engineer using it. Many developers maintain a profile because they feel they have to, not because it represents them well.

Portfolio tools and identity experiments have tried to thread this needle — read.cv was a genuine attempt before it was acquired and wound down — but none solved the underlying structural problem: they were standalone products with no built-in community, no critical mass, and no clear reason for engineers to show up and stay.

The most specific failure, though, is something none of these platforms addressed at all. No mainstream tool supports self-reported skill levels in a structured, filterable way. You can list technologies on LinkedIn, but you cannot say you are a senior-level Elixir engineer with intermediate Rust and a year of production Phoenix experience. That granularity — the kind that actually helps someone decide whether to reach out — simply does not exist anywhere in a form designed for discovery.

Stack Overflow's annual developer survey captures self-reported skill data at scale — but in aggregate, not as individual profiles. The signal exists; there is just no surface to make it discoverable.
Part II

A Different Vision: Share and Be Found

What would a developer profile look like if it were designed purely for honest self-presentation and quiet discoverability — not for engagement, not for networking theater, not for an algorithm to optimize? The answer turns out to be quite simple, which is part of why it hasn't been built: simple things are hard to monetize on their own.

The core object is a structured profile. You declare your overall level — junior, mid-level, senior, architect — and list the technologies you work with, each tagged with your self-assessed competence. You add a handful of projects with brief descriptions and optional links. You note your location and whether you are open to opportunities. That is it. No feed, no followers, no endorsements from colleagues who want something in return. The profile is a document — stable, editable, yours — not a performance.

Discovery is one-directional by design. Someone searching for a senior Elixir engineer in a specific city finds you through filters; you do not need to do anything to be found except keep your profile accurate. There is no social graph to tend, no connection requests to manage. The asymmetry is intentional: it removes the overhead that makes LinkedIn exhausting while preserving the thing that makes it useful — the ability to be found by someone who needs what you offer.

The profile data should be yours to take with you. Exportable, deletable, not held hostage to a platform's survival or business model pivot. This is not a technical requirement so much as a design commitment — a statement about whose interests the platform actually serves. Engineers who have been burned by acquisitions and shutdowns know the difference.

Part III

The Shape of the Thing

The profile is the atomic unit. Name, location with granularity controls, a short bio, overall skill level, a technology list with self-reported levels, and a project list with brief descriptions. Optional fields — availability status, preferred work type, contact preference — round it out without demanding it. Nothing more is required to be present and findable.

The filters are the product. A hiring manager or a fellow developer can search by location, technology, skill level, and availability. The results are profiles — human-readable, not ranked by follower count or engagement score. The idea that a company looking for a specific kind of engineer should be able to find one without a recruiter in the loop is not radical; it is just not well-served by anything that currently exists.

The trust model is deliberately minimal. Everything is self-reported. There is no verification system, no endorsement mechanism, no badge that proves you are what you say you are. The incentive for honesty is structural: overstating your level leads to interviews you cannot pass, and the platform's value erodes if profiles are inaccurate. Verification theater — the kind LinkedIn engages in with its skill assessments — adds friction without adding meaningful signal. The form design does more work than the machinery of attestation.

The one place where external signal matters: linking a GitHub account or a public project adds weight to claimed skills without requiring a formal verification step. Optional, low-friction, meaningful.

Profile pages are public and indexable. That is a feature. Engineers should be findable by search engines, not just by people who already know the platform. Contact information, however, is gated — visible only to registered users — which is sufficient to deter most automated harvesting without walling off the directory entirely.

Part IV

Why This Is Not a Standalone Product

A developer directory with no members is worth nothing. That is the whole problem. LinkedIn's moat is not its features — its features are widely disliked — it is the accumulated presence of hundreds of millions of professionals who are already there. Building a competing directory from scratch, without an existing audience, is a cold-start problem that no amount of good design solves on its own.

This is a secondary product. It works when it is attached to a community that already has the trust of working engineers — one where developers show up regularly, identify with the brand, and have already established some shared context about what they know and how they work. The directory becomes valuable the moment that community's membership is large enough and specific enough that the filters produce meaningful results. It does not need to compete with LinkedIn globally; it needs to be genuinely useful within a defined population.

The business model follows from this logic. For individual engineers, the profile is free — permanently, not as a loss-leader. The value to the host organization is different: a searchable, curated directory of their community's members is something employers will pay for access to, particularly when they already trust the community's signal. That is a cleaner transaction than a recruiter's commission, and it keeps the incentive structure legible to everyone involved.

The precedent is media companies that built subscriber-only communities and found, over time, that the community itself was more valuable than the content that seeded it. The directory is that community's professional surface — made useful to the outside world without compromising what made it worth joining.

The right organization to build this already has most of what it needs: an engaged membership of developers who trust it, a reputation built on quality rather than scale, and — if it has been paying attention — inbound interest from employers who want to hire from that specific pool. The missing piece is not technical. It is the decision to offer a structured professional surface to a community that has, until now, existed only as an audience.

Whether that decision gets made is not a product question. It is a strategic one. But the engineers are already there, the employers are already asking, and the gap between those two facts is exactly the size of this idea.
